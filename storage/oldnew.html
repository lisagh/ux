Newfields

Explore more than 54,000 works of art in Newfields with the Wayfinding App.

Background

We collaborated with Newfields to design a wayfinding app. Newfields is a cultural heritage campus in Indianapolis that includes the Indianapolis Museum of Art (IMA) and other culturally and historically significant places. The campus surfaces over 152 square acres and boasting 54,000 works of art that cross 5,000 years of art history.

Design Process

Problem Space

Newfields is continually looking for opportunities to improve the experience of their guests. A survey conducted by Newfields identified “wayfinding” as the top area for improvement.

Research

We reviewed the data about museum visitors from the internal resources, which helped us have an initial understanding of the future user.

Meet the client

In this project, we worked closely with Newfiedls IT department. After talking to engineers about existing technology, we interviewed the Executive Director, Marketing, Hospitality, Facilities, and some other related museum staff as well.

Previous Next

Based on these interviews, the expectations of this app from our client are:

1. Assist with visitor wayfinding helping to navigate the physical environment and enhancing their understanding and experience
2. Add to visitor engagement with the art pieces by involving them in activity beyond simply viewing an art piece

Talk to Future Users

It's not enough for us to only have conversations with the stakeholders, we were more concern about our future users. There are six contextual interviews performed by the team. We invited all of the participants to visit the Newfields at different times, and one of the team members would observe and record their journey.
Here is how it works:

The insights of contextual interviews:

1. Guests can get very lost in the galleries.
2. Guests frequently want to know more about specific art pieces.

Arrange Information

To begin modeling the guest, we created an affinity diagram. We reviewed our notes from the contextual inquiries and business stakeholder interviews to pull out information, ideas, opinions, and issues.
Note: White notes from the guest and grey notes from the stakeholders.

We identified the following design requirements:

1. A guest should be able to use the application to explore the museum.
2. Guests should be able to use the application to determine their current location within the museum.
3. Guests should be able to use the application to find a specific exhibit.
4. The application should require minimal interaction, thereby allowing the guest to focus on the environment.

Potential users

We created four different personas to represent the potential users. They are the parents, travelers, art lovers, and movable middle. The movable middle is a group of people can be future visitors but not yet been.The components include basic demographic information, goals, frustrations, and a bio that provides more details of each persona. In the design process we use these personas to generate empathy and to better understand the demographics that we are designing for.

Take the Journey

We use the customer journey map here to describe the scenario from the beginning when the user enters the museum to the end, leaves the museum. The scenario implies the observation of the current user experience. We listed all the possible user activities in chronological order.

Brainstorm & Conceptual Design

With the user models we were able to sit in a design workshop and brainstorm about what possible technology solutions could be created to meet Newfields business needs as well as their visitor’s needs. We were able to generate a lot of ideas and then began narrowing them down. We went through recurring themes in our data and figured out how we could best solve for the user needs while also meeting the business needs. Our ideas took shape in many different form factors such as:

Our workshop yielded great results as far as ideas, but staying in compliance with client needs and user expectation was our primary goal. With that said, we narrowed down our options to a mobile application, an interactive kiosk, and augmented reality glasses. Each of these design solutions will be able to meet many of the business needs while also addressing the users wants. At the heart of all the designs was the mobile app.

Let's play it

Evaluation

We invited seven people to participate the final interactive prototype evaluation. They visited the museum and finished five different tasks

1. indicating initial preferences
2. removing a marker from the map
3. searching for an art piece
4. navigating to an art piece
5. viewing another floor of the museum

Observations and Comments
Some participants were confused by the onboarding screens, including how many selections they could make. An example comment: “I didn’t know what the pictures meant.”
When adding and removing markers from the map, participants commented that it would be helpful to provide a special indication in the map for the marker that was just added or removed.
Searching for a specific exhibit appeared to be very easy and intuitive. An example comment: “I liked that it showed the gallery spaces and where everything was located.”
When navigating to an art piece, participants like getting notifications when they neared map markers, but recommended that the notifications include a picture of the art piece.

Lessons learned

I got a very good experience in this project. My teammates and I walked into the museum, talked to people, discussed possibilities, explored different ideas and iterated designs. The feedback from different users sometimes conflicts with each other, what I need to do, as a UX designer is to figure out why and how and try to balance the needs.
